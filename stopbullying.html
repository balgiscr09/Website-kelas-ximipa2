How to Stop Bullying in This School

What is Bullying?

You can usually identify bullying through the following three characteristics: intent, repetition, and power. A person who bullies intends to cause pain, either through physical harm or hurtful words or behavior, and does so repeatedly. Boys are more likely to experience physical bullying, while girls are more likely to experience psychological bullying. Bullying can happen both in person or online. Cyberbullying often occurs over social media, SMS/text or instant message, email, or any online platform where children interact.

Types of Bullying

Physical bullying: Hitting, pushing, kicking, spitting, damaging or stealing belongings, making threats of violence.

Verbal bullying: Name-calling or insulting someone, threatening someone, making mean jokes or put-downs.

Social (relational) bullying: Deliberately excluding someone from a group, spreading rumors or gossip, embarrassing someone in public, damaging a person's social reputation.

Cyberbullying: Using electronic devices to harass someone, sending hurtful messages, images, or videos, spreading rumors or lies through email, social media, or text messages.

Psychological bullying: Can overlap with social bullying and may include intimidation, stalking, or manipulation through dirty looks or constant criticism.

Sexual bullying: Includes any physical, verbal, or online bullying that is sexual in nature, such as unwanted touching, making sexual jokes or gestures, or sharing explicit images or rumors.

Non-verbal abuse: Includes actions like using hostile hand signs or text messages that aren't explicitly verbal but are still abusive.

Signs of Being Bullied

Physical marks such as unexplained bruises, scratches, broken bones, and healing wounds.

Fear of going to school or joining school events.

Being anxious, nervous, or very vigilant.

Having few friends in school or outside of school.

Losing friends suddenly or avoiding social situations.

Clothing, electronics, or other personal belongings being lost or destroyed.

Often asking for money.

Low academic performance.

Absenteeism or calling from school asking to go home.

Trying to stay near adults.

Not sleeping well and may be having nightmares.

Complaining of headaches, stomach aches, or other physical ailments.

Regularly distressed after spending time online or on their phone.

Becomes unusually secretive, especially regarding online activities.

Being aggressive or having angry outbursts.

The Shadow of Bullying in Indonesia’s Education System

According to Universitas Muhammadiyah Surakarta (March 13, 2024)

Behind the veil of Indonesian education, a deep wound persists, affecting the younger generation: bullying. This phenomenon is a parasite, making kids feel unsafe and inflicting deep trauma on them.

A 2022 study by the Ministry of Education and Culture found that 36.31% of students could be experiencing bullying, whether through words, actions, or online. However, only 13.54% felt brave enough to speak up about it. The United Federation of Indonesian Teachers (FGSI) recorded an increase in bullying cases from 21 cases in 2022 to 30 in 2023. Meanwhile, the Indonesian Child Protection Commission (KPAI) reported 87 bullying cases out of 2,355 total reports of child abuse.

Recent violent incidents at schools, including the tragic death of a student at an Islamic boarding school, have highlighted the urgent need for preventive action. Schools should be safe spaces for learning, not environments for fear and harm.

Viral Elementary School Student’s Suicide in Banyuwangi Reveals the Dangers of Bullying

According to Muhammadiyah University of Surabaya (March 6, 2023)

Social media was shocked by the suicide of an 11-year-old student from Banyuwangi, East Java, suspected to be a victim of bullying for being called an orphan. Uswatun Hasanah, Lecturer in Mental Nursing at UM Surabaya, explained that bullying is an intentional and repeated act that intimidates or threatens victims.

She emphasized that bullying can cause victims to lose confidence, develop anxiety or depression, and even have suicidal thoughts. Victims may also experience academic decline and social withdrawal. Perpetrators, on the other hand, risk becoming aggressive and antisocial if left unchecked.

Uswatun called on parents, schools, and the government to strengthen supervision and policies to prevent further cases.

Viral Bullying in Blora Middle School Bathroom

According to Detiknews (November 11, 2025)

A video of a bullying incident at a Blora junior high school went viral, showing a student being beaten and mocked by classmates in a school bathroom. Police handled 33 students for questioning and provided educational coaching rather than punishment. Parents were summoned to cooperate with the investigation.

KPAI member Dian Sasmita stressed that bullying should not be normalized as mere jokes, as every act of bullying—physical or online—can cause serious psychological harm. She urged parents, educators, and policymakers to understand the impact and respond appropriately.

Six Students in South Bekasi Traumatized After Bullying Incident

According to Wartakotalive (October 16, 2025)

A viral video showed six students from SMPN 1 Tambun Selatan, Bekasi Regency, being bullied by classmates. Police conducted medical examinations and provided psychological support for the victims. The Women’s Empowerment and Child Protection Agency (DP3A) was also involved in handling the case.

Investigations revealed that both perpetrators and victims were from the same school. The incident occurred on September 8, 2025, and authorities continue to assist families and ensure such events do not repeat.

Bullying Case in Depok Junior High School Focuses on Psychological Recovery

According to Berita Depok (October 4, 2024)

Following a bullying case involving a special needs student at SMPN 8 Depok, the Depok City DP3AP2KB took immediate action by visiting the victim at the hospital. The department promised continuous psychological assistance for the student and educational programs for the school community.

Head of DP3AP2KB, Nessi Annisa Handari, stated that the main focus is on recovery and prevention through collaboration with the local education office. The initiative aims to build a more inclusive school environment.

Chronology of Alleged Bullying Leading to Death at Geyer Junior High School

According to Tempo (October 14, 2025)

A seventh-grade student at SMPN 1 Geyer, Grobogan, Central Java, died after a fight allegedly caused by bullying. The victim, Angga Bagus Perwira, reportedly slipped and hit his head after a scuffle with a classmate. Police and the Ministry of Education are investigating the case, which drew attention from the House of Representatives Commission X.

Lawmakers called for stricter enforcement of Regulation No. 46 of 2023 on preventing violence in educational institutions, urging schools to handle such cases transparently and prioritize victims’ welfare.

Teacher Fear Contributes to Bullying Cases, Says DPR Deputy Speaker

According to Kompas (October 16, 2025)

Deputy DPR Speaker Cucun Ahmad Syamsurijal stated that teachers’ fear of intervention contributes to recurring bullying cases, including the death of ABP, a 12-year-old student in Grobogan. Many teachers hesitate to act for fear of legal repercussions. Cucun urged better protection for teachers and curriculum reforms to strengthen character education.

He added that teachers need authority to supervise classrooms effectively and prevent conflicts. The statement followed reports that ABP had long been bullied before his death, underscoring weak school supervision.

How to Prevent and Stop Bullying

Speak up: Victims or witnesses should tell a teacher, parent, or trusted adult so the bullying can be stopped early.

Support victims: Show kindness and don’t join in or laugh at the bully’s actions. Include victims in activities to help them feel less alone.

Educate: Teach students about respect, empathy, and the impact of bullying.

Set rules: Schools should make clear anti-bullying rules and consequences.

Get help: Victims and bullies may both need counseling to recover or change behavior.

Understand motivation: Realize bullies often project their own insecurities; their words reflect them, not you.

Don’t respond with hate: Stay calm and avoid arguing, as reacting gives them control.

Set emotional boundaries: Protect your mental health by blocking or reporting harmful accounts.

Promote positive spaces: Spread kindness and supportive messages online and offline.

Be assertive: If safe, tell the bully to stop directly and confidently.

Walk away: If it continues, leave and find a safe place or adult.

Stay with others: Avoid being alone with bullies and stick with friends.

Encourage open communication: Create environments where students can share concerns freely.

Model kindness: Treat others with respect and kindness to inspire a supportive culture.

Build confidence: Encourage victims to pursue hobbies that boost self-esteem and social connections.
