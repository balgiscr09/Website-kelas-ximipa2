UNESCO Has Designated Raja Ampat as A World Biosphere Reserve

According to TEMPO (September 27, 2025)

The United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (UNESCO) designated Raja Ampat as a world biosphere reserve on Saturday, September 27, 2025. The area in Southwest Papua Province is considered to have a marine ecosystem with the highest biodiversity in the world.

The Head of the West Papua Natural Resources Conservation Agency, Johny Santoso, stated that Papua's land conservation is strongly supported by the local wisdom of indigenous communities, particularly those in Raja Ampat Regency. "'The forest is mother, the sea is father,' is the spirit of the Raja Ampat people to protect their forests and their seas," he said during the proposal for the Raja Ampat Biosphere Reserve in Waisai on September 24, 2024, as recorded in a UNESCO release received by Tempo.

According to Johny, there are nine conservation areas in Raja Ampat Regency that are managed collaboratively by indigenous communities, local and central governments, non-governmental organizations, academics, and other stakeholders. These nine areas form the core of the Raja Ampat Biosphere Reserve.

After being designated a UNESCO Global Geopark in 2023, Raja Ampat is now also recognized as a biosphere reserve. With this dual status, Raja Ampat joins the very limited number of regions worldwide to receive dual UNESCO recognition.

Avoiding Fiscal Exhaustion

According to A. Prasetyo, Kompas.id (November 4, 2025)

One of the debates welcoming the one-year anniversary of the Prabowo Subianto‚ÄìGibran Rakabuming administration is whether our fiscal situation remains healthy. Finance Minister Purbaya Yudhi Sadewa claims it is secure. The reason is that the government's budget deficit is still below 3 percent of the gross domestic product. Meanwhile, the ratio of government debt balance is still around 40 percent of GDP, which is well below the legal ceiling of 60 percent. The 3 percent deficit limit and 60 percent debt balance were adopted from the Maastricht Treaty (1992), an agreement among European Union countries when they were about to issue the common currency, the euro. This standard was explicitly formalized by the Government of Indonesia in Law Number 17 of 2003 concerning State Finance.

Currently, this criterion has been abandoned by European Union countries and other developed nations, especially since the onset of the crisis storm, ranging from the global financial crisis (2008) to the Covid-19 pandemic crisis (2020). Now, almost all developed countries have a very large government debt balance ratio and a wide government budget deficit. Japan has become the country with the highest debt-to-GDP ratio in the world, at approximately 230 percent. In Europe, Greece holds the highest record with a debt ratio exceeding 150 percent of GDP. This is followed by Italy, Portugal, and Belgium, with debt ratios ranging from 100 to 140 percent of GDP. Meanwhile, the debt ratio of the United States reaches 124 percent of GDP. Compared to those countries, Indonesia's debt ratio is still very small. However, is our fiscal condition truly secure and sustainable? In 2011, senior economist at the International Monetary Fund, Atish R. Ghosh, popularized the concept of fiscal fatigue as a more detailed indicator of debt's role in increasing economic productivity.

Over the past ten years, the Indonesian government's debt ratio has increased dramatically in line with increased infrastructure spending. In 2014, our debt ratio was still around 30 percent of GDP. By 2024, it had reached 38.8 percent of GDP. The issue is that the increase in the debt ratio is not in line with the increase in investment. The investment ratio to the economy has only slightly risen, from 32.5 percent in 2014 to 33 percent in 2024. This means that the debt ratio has surged by nearly 9 percentage points, while the investment ratio has only increased by 0.5 percentage points. Investment stagnation is accompanied by a decline in manufacturing sector activities. The manufacturing ratio to GDP as an indicator has decreased from 21 percent in 2014 to 18 percent in 2024. In fact, in 2005, the role of the manufacturing sector in the economy once reached 28 percent. Over the past twenty years, the role of the manufacturing sector has declined by approximately 10 percent. Tax revenue has also experienced stagnation. In 2014, the tax-to-GDP ratio reached 11.4 percent. In 2024, the ratio is only 10 percent. In 2008, the tax ratio once reached its highest figure, 13 percent. Of course, this is with the note that there were external factors at the time, such as the commodity boom. Domestic factors such as stagnation in investment and the decline in the role of the manufacturing sector in recent years have been the main contributors to the decrease in state revenue.

Several indicators can identify signs of fiscal exhaustion. First, the primary budget balance, or the difference between revenues and expenditures, excluding debt payments. A positive primary budget balance means the government can pay its debts from revenues. A negative balance means the government needs to issue new debt to service the debt. We once experienced a peak in the primary budget surplus in 2008 at 1.7 percent due to soaring commodity prices. Since 2011, we have begun to experience a primary budget deficit that continues to this day. By 2025, the deficit is projected to reach 0.5 percent of GDP. The Covid-19 pandemic has indeed worsened the quality of our primary budget balance. However, massive infrastructure development, including the Jakarta-Bandung high-speed train (Whoosh) and the new national capital in East Kalimantan, has also made a significant contribution. Second, the ratio of debt interest payments to state revenue. In 2024, the ratio reaches 17 percent. In contrast, in 2014, the ratio was still 8.5 percent. Thus, over the past decade, there has been a significant increase in the burden of debt interest payments that has not been accompanied by a sufficient increase in economic capacity and performance. Third, spending rigidity is due to the large number of mandatory spending items. In 2014, mandatory spending accounted for approximately 60 percent. By 2024, the allocation will exceed 70 percent of total annual spending (the state budget). The greater the mandatory spending, the narrower the opportunity to increase dynamic sectoral productivity.

The Prabowo-Gibran administration inherits a challenging fiscal condition. Therefore, the government's future programs must pay attention to the effectiveness and efficiency of the budget to avoid exacerbating the already strained fiscal situation. The key lies how each government expenditure can optimally enhance economic productivity through increased investment activities, particularly in the manufacturing sector. The 2026 State Budget Law indicates a substantial populist expenditure, namely the Free Nutritious Food program (Rp 335 trillion), the Red and White Village Cooperative (Rp 83 trillion), and public housing (Rp 57.7 trillion). The total of these three flagship programs accounts for 12 percent of the government's total budget. President Prabowo's vision of revitalizing the public consumption sector (demand side) is highly relevant to our economic situation, with its vast number of vulnerable groups. However, without adequate institutional arrangements, such substantial fiscal spending will simply evaporate, while equity deteriorates. The goal of development is not merely to increase growth (welfare), but also to promote justice (justice). Amartya Sen, the 1998 Nobel Prize-winning economic philosopher, once warned that there is no prosperity without justice. We must not allow society to seek its own path to justice, leading to chaos that would undermine the ideals of a Golden Indonesia 2045.

Worker Trapped For 11 Hours After Collapse of Medieval Tower in Central Rome Dies

According to Bill Hutchinson, Phoebe Natanson, Jon Haworth, ABC News (November 4, 2025)

A worker who had been trapped for 11 hours in the partial collapse of a medieval tower in the heart of Rome has died after being rescued late Monday, officials said. The 66-year-old man, Octay Stroici, was the last of several workers saved in the rescue operation that was briefly interrupted by a second collapse of the 95-foot-tall Torre de' Conti tower, Luca Cari, a spokesperson for the Rome Fire Service, told ABC News. The man's condition had been reported as critical when rescuers got him down to the ambulance before the drive to the hospital.

"On behalf of myself and the government, I express my deepest sorrow and condolences for the tragic death of Octay Stroici, the worker who fell victim to the collapse of the Torre dei Conti in Rome," read a statement from Prime Minister Georgia Meloni's office that was released overnight. "Our thoughts are with his family and colleagues at this time of unspeakable grief. I would like to thank once again the rescue workers and all those who worked tirelessly and courageously to try to save his life."

Before being saved, the trapped worker was conscious and communicating with search-and-rescue crews, Cari said. About 140 firefighters responded to the scene, some digging with their bare hands to free the worker.

Rome Mayor Roberto Gualtieri told reporters on Monday evening, just before the last rescue was made, that doctors had reached the worker and supplied him with oxygen. No firefighters were injured in the incident, officials said. The tower is in the historic part of central Rome near the Roman Forum and the Colosseum.

Cari said the emergency unfolded around 11 a.m. local time. At the time of the first collapse, 11 workers from two companies were working on the tower. "It all happened suddenly," one of the workers told Italy's ANSA news agency. "Then I only saw the cloud of dust and the rescuers." Cari said the second partial collapse occurred about an hour after the first, while firefighters were attempting to rescue the workers.

At least three other workers were pulled from the rubble, two of them unharmed, officials said. One rescued worker, a 64-year-old man, was taken to a hospital with a head injury, officials said. The cause of the collapse remains under investigation. The tower has been closed to the public since 2007, but recently received a large grant from Italy's National Recovery and Resilience Plan for the renovation, officials said. The Torre dei Conti tower, which dates back to the 13th century, was built by Pope Innocent III as a residence for his family.
